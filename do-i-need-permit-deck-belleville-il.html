Quick Answer by Situation

The short answer for most Belleville-area homeowners is yes, you need a permit. Here are the common scenarios:

⚠ Permit Required

Attached deck of any size — connected to your house requires a permit in virtually every Metro East municipality.

⚠ Permit Required

Deck more than 30 inches off the ground — height above grade triggers permit requirements in Belleville, O'Fallon, and most surrounding cities.

⚠ Permit Required

New deck over 200 square feet — in most municipalities, a new freestanding deck over 200 sq ft requires a permit.

? May Not Require Permit

Freestanding deck under 200 sq ft, under 30 inches high — may be exempt in some cities, but rules vary. Always verify with your city before assuming you're exempt.

✓ Usually No Permit Needed

Replacing deck boards only — deck board replacement with no structural changes typically does not require a permit. If you're changing structure, footings, or footprint, the rule changes.

When in doubt, get the permit. A $200–$400 permit is a small price compared to the consequences of building without one. All-Pro handles permit applications on every project — it's included in our process at no extra charge.

What Happens If You Build Without a Permit?

This is the part most homeowners don't think about until it's too late:

Stop-work orders and fines

If the city discovers unpermitted work in progress, they issue a stop-work order and fines. You may be required to uncover the work so inspectors can see the structure — including digging up footings.

Required demolition

In serious cases, the city can require you to tear down the deck and rebuild it with proper permits. This is rare, but it happens — and it's entirely preventable.

Problems when you sell

This is the most common consequence. When you list your home, the buyer's inspector or title company will discover the unpermitted structure. You'll either have to disclose it, retroactively permit it (if possible), or knock it down before closing. We've seen this derail sales.

Insurance issues

If someone is injured on an unpermitted deck, your homeowner's insurance may deny the claim. The unpermitted status can be used to argue the structure was unsafe and non-compliant.

Bottom line: The permit isn't bureaucratic paperwork for its own sake. It protects you — legally, financially, and when you sell. Always hire a contractor who pulls permits. All-Pro does, on every job.

Who Should Pull the Permit — You or Your Contractor?

Your contractor should pull the permit. This is non-negotiable on a legitimate job.

When a contractor pulls the permit, they take responsibility for the work meeting code. When you pull it yourself, the liability shifts to you as the homeowner-builder. Some contractors ask homeowners to pull their own permits because:

  • They're not licensed or properly insured to pull permits in that city
  • They want to avoid inspection scrutiny
  • They're cutting corners and hoping you don't notice

If a contractor tells you to pull your own permit, walk away. All-Pro has pulled permits in Belleville, O'Fallon, Edwardsville, Fairview Heights, Collinsville, and 30+ other Metro East cities. We know the process, we handle it, and you get the permit number before we start.
