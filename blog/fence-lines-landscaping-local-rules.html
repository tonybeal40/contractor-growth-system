Fence Lines, Landscaping, and Local Rules: Our Opinion Before You Start

By Bill Session · June 9, 2026 · Fence and Landscaping Guidance

Outdoor work gets expensive when homeowners skip the boring checks. In our opinion, fences, retaining edges, trees, drainage changes, and landscaping near property lines deserve extra attention before materials are ordered or posts are set.

Important: This article is not legal advice. We are not your attorney, surveyor, HOA, or city inspector. Always verify property lines, easements, utility locations, HOA rules, and permit requirements with the proper local authority before work begins.

Know the Line Before You Build Near It

A fence or landscape bed that looks obvious from the yard may not match the legal property line. Old posts, tree rows, and neighbor mowing patterns are not the same as a survey. If the job is close to a property line, our opinion is to slow down and verify before building.

That matters for fence installation, drainage changes, trees, and hardscape work. A clean start protects the homeowner, the neighbor relationship, and the project timeline.

Check Access and Easements

Outdoor projects can block access for utility work, drainage, meters, gates, or future repairs. Before installing a fence, planting dense landscaping, or building near the side yard, homeowners should think through who may need access later.

  • Call utility locate services before digging.
  • Check HOA or subdivision rules when applicable.
  • Ask the city or county about permits and height limits.
  • Keep gates, meters, drainage paths, and service access usable.

Good Contractors Ask These Questions Early

A contractor should not just ask what style you want. They should ask where the line is, what access needs to remain open, how water moves, and whether local rules may affect the work. That is part of why we push homeowners toward written estimates and clear scopes.
