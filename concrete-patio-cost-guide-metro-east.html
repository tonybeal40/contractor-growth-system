Plain Concrete vs Stamped vs Pavers: Which Is Right for You?

Plain Concrete (Broom Finish)

  • Cheapest option — $8–$14/sq ft installed
  • Very durable and low maintenance — sweep and occasional sealing
  • Clean, simple look that works with any home style
  • Seal every 2–3 years to protect from Illinois freeze/thaw cycles
  • Best for: budget-conscious projects, utilitarian spaces, garages/driveways doubling as patios

Stamped Concrete

  • Mid-range cost — $14–$22/sq ft installed
  • Mimics the look of stone, slate, brick, or wood — without the material cost
  • More slippery when wet than broom finish — important for pool areas
  • Sealing is critical (every 1–2 years) to protect color and pattern
  • If it cracks, repairs are more visible than plain concrete
  • Best for: homeowners who want premium look at mid-range cost

Pavers

  • Higher upfront cost — $14–$30/sq ft installed depending on material
  • If one section settles or heaves, you can lift and re-level individual pavers
  • Huge design flexibility — patterns, mixed colors, borders
  • Requires periodic re-sanding of joints to prevent weeds and shifting
  • Best for: design-focused projects, areas prone to settling, phased projects

Our honest take: For most Metro East homeowners, plain or stamped concrete gives the best value. Pavers are worth it if you want premium aesthetics or have a yard with drainage/settling issues where concrete is more likely to crack.

What Changes the Price

Size and shape

Simple rectangular slabs cost less than curved, irregular, or multi-section designs. Complex shapes require more labor to form and cut.

Existing concrete removal

If you have an existing slab that needs to come out, add $3–$7 per square foot for break-out and haul-away. A 400 sq ft existing slab adds $1,200–$2,800 to the project.

Excavation and base prep

Metro East clay soil requires thorough excavation, grading, and compacted gravel base to prevent heaving and cracking. Skipping proper base prep is the number one cause of premature patio failure in Illinois.

Reinforcement

Rebar or wire mesh reinforcement adds $1–$2/sq ft but significantly reduces cracking risk. We recommend it on all patios over 200 sq ft and anywhere with heavy use.

Drainage

Patios near the house need to slope away properly (1/4 inch per foot minimum). If drainage is complicated by the existing grade, French drains or channel drains add cost but protect your foundation.

Illinois Climate: What to Know About Concrete in Metro East

Metro East is tough on concrete. Here's what matters for longevity:

  • Freeze/thaw cycles — water seeps into small cracks, freezes, expands, and widens them. Sealing prevents water infiltration and dramatically extends patio life.
  • Clay soil — expands when wet, contracts when dry. Proper base prep (4–6 inches of compacted gravel) is essential to prevent heaving.
  • De-icing salt — salt is corrosive to concrete surfaces. Use sand instead of salt near patios, or use calcium chloride if needed (less damaging than rock salt).
  • Sealing schedule — plain concrete: seal every 3 years. Stamped: seal every 1–2 years. The small cost of sealing is a fraction of the cost of premature cracking.

How to Get an Accurate Concrete Patio Quote

Know your approximate size

Even a rough sketch with dimensions helps contractors give you a more accurate quote. Know if you want stairs, borders, or specialty finishes before calling.

Get written, itemized quotes

A written quote should specify: square footage, concrete thickness, reinforcement included, finish type, sealer included, and whether old concrete removal is in scope.

Ask about base prep specifics

Ask: "How many inches of gravel base will be included?" The answer should be 4–6 inches of compacted gravel. Contractors who cut corners on base prep deliver patios that crack early.

Watch for these red flags

  • Quote given without visiting the site
  • No mention of base prep or gravel depth
  • Cash-only payment required
  • No written contract with scope detail
  • Price is dramatically lower than all other quotes (usually means shortcuts)
