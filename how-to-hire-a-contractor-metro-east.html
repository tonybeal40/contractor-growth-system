Most contractor horror stories follow the same pattern. Here's how to avoid them — what to ask, what to watch for, and what a good contractor actually looks like.

Before You Call Anyone

The homeowners who get the best results aren't the ones who call the first number they find. They do 15 minutes of prep first. Here's what that looks like:

  • Know your rough scope. You don't need exact measurements, but "12x16 deck with stairs" is better than "a deck." The clearer you are, the more accurate the quote.
  • Set a realistic budget range. Not to tell the contractor — but so you know if a quote is in the ballpark or wildly off. Use our deck cost guide as a reference.
  • Know your timeline. Need it done before July 4th? Say so upfront. Good contractors book out 4–8 weeks.
  • Look up reviews before you call. Google, HomeAdvisor, and Angi all show verified reviews. Look for patterns — not just star count.

Questions to Ask Every Contractor

Ask these before you let anyone start measuring. A good contractor answers all of these without hesitation.

"Are you licensed and insured in Illinois?"
Illinois doesn't require a statewide general contractor license, but ask for proof of general liability insurance and workers compensation coverage. If they can't provide it, you're financially exposed if someone gets hurt on your property.
"Do you pull the permits, or do I?"
Most permitted work — decks, additions, structural remodels — requires a building permit. A good contractor handles this. If they say "you handle the permit," that's a yellow flag. If they say "you don't need a permit" when you clearly do, that's a red flag.
"Will the owner be on the job site?"
Some companies quote the job, then hand it to a subcontractor you've never met. Ask directly if the person you're talking to will be present during work. This matters more than most homeowners realize.
"Can you give me 2 references from similar recent projects?"
Not just names — phone numbers of people you can actually call. Ask the references: Did they stay on schedule? Did the final bill match the quote? Would you hire them again?
"What does the payment schedule look like?"
Reasonable: 10–30% deposit, progress payments tied to milestones, final payment at completion. Unreasonable: 50%+ before work starts, or full payment in cash before the job is done.
"What happens if the project goes over budget or over schedule?"
Change orders happen. The question is whether you'll be informed before costs increase, or surprised at the end. Good answer: "We document any changes in writing and get your approval before proceeding."

How to Compare Quotes

Getting multiple quotes is smart — but only if you're comparing the same thing. Here's how to do it right:

Always get written quotes

A verbal estimate is not a quote. It's a conversation. Written quotes show the scope — materials, dimensions, labor, exclusions — so you can see exactly what you're getting for the price.

Compare scope, not just price

A $12,000 quote and a $16,000 quote might look very different on paper but be quoting completely different scopes. Check:

  • Same size and dimensions?
  • Same materials (PT wood vs composite, wood vs vinyl fence)?
  • Permits included or excluded?
  • Cleanup and haul-away included?
  • Same number of stairs, railings, features?

Cheapest quote is often not the best value

The lowest bid usually means something was left out of scope, cheaper materials are being used, or the contractor is cutting corners on labor. If one quote is 40% lower than the others, ask why specifically — not as a negotiation, but to understand what's different.

Real example: A homeowner gets three deck quotes — $10k, $14k, $16k. The $10k quote doesn't include permit fees, haul-away of the old deck, or stair railings. After add-ons, it ends up at $15,500. The $14k quote was all-in. The "cheapest" option wasn't.

Red Flags — Walk Away Immediately

These are the warning signs Metro East homeowners have reported to us after bad experiences with other contractors:

  • Requires more than 30–40% cash deposit before work begins
  • Only accepts cash — no checks or card
  • Can't provide proof of insurance when asked
  • No written contract or estimate — just a handshake
  • Says you don't need a permit when the project clearly requires one
  • No local address, no local history, no verifiable reviews
  • Shows up unsolicited after a storm offering discounted work ("storm chasers")
  • Pressures you to sign today or the price goes up
  • Can't give you a single reference from recent local work
  • Goes silent after the deposit is paid

What Your Contract Should Include

Before any work starts, you should have a signed written agreement that includes:

  • Full scope of work — what exactly is being built or installed
  • Materials list — brand, grade, dimensions, and quantities
  • Start date and estimated completion date
  • Total price and payment schedule with milestone triggers
  • Change order process — how changes are documented and priced
  • Who is responsible for permits and inspections
  • Cleanup and haul-away responsibilities
  • Warranty on workmanship (separate from material manufacturer warranties)
  • Contractor's license/insurance information

If a contractor won't put it in writing, don't hire them. The contract protects you AND them. Any reputable contractor understands this.

Payment Schedule Guidance

This is where many homeowners get into trouble. Here's what fair looks like:

Payment When Reasonable Amount
Deposit At contract signing 10–30% (covers materials order)
Progress payment After framing/major milestone 30–40% of total
Final payment After final walkthrough and your approval Remaining balance

Never pay the final balance before you've walked the completed project and are satisfied. A reputable contractor expects this.

Permits — Who's Responsible

Many homeowners don't realize they share responsibility for permits. Here's how it works in Illinois:

  • The permit is tied to the work, not just the contractor. If work is done without a required permit, the homeowner can be fined and required to tear it out.
  • When you sell your home, unpermitted work can kill a deal or reduce your sale price.
  • Ask your contractor: "Will you pull the permit in your name, and can you show me the permit number before work starts?"
  • You can verify permits at your city's building department website or by calling the permit office directly.
